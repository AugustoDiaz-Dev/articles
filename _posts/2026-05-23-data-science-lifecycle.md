The Data Science Lifecycle is a structured workflow that helps transform a business problem into useful insights, models, and decisions. It gives data scientists a repeatable path from understanding the problem to communicating the final results.

![Data Science Lifecycle]({{ "/assets/data-science-lifecycle.png" | relative_url }})

## 1. Business Understanding

Every data science project should begin with a clear business question. Before collecting data or building models, we need to understand the objective, the stakeholders, and what success looks like.

## 2. Data Mining

Once the problem is clear, the next step is gathering the required data. This can include databases, APIs, spreadsheets, web scraping, or external datasets.

## 3. Data Cleaning

Raw data is usually incomplete, inconsistent, or noisy. Data cleaning includes fixing missing values, removing duplicates, correcting errors, and preparing the data for analysis.

## 4. Data Exploration

Exploratory Data Analysis helps us understand patterns, relationships, outliers, and possible assumptions in the data.

## 5. Feature Engineering

Feature engineering transforms raw data into useful inputs for machine learning models. Good features often improve model performance more than complex algorithms.

## 6. Predictive Modeling

In this stage, machine learning models are trained, evaluated, and improved. The goal is not just accuracy, but usefulness for the original business problem.

## 7. Data Visualization

The final step is communicating insights clearly. Visualizations, dashboards, and reports help stakeholders understand results and make decisions.

## Conclusion

The Data Science Lifecycle is not always linear. Teams often move back and forth between steps as they learn more about the data and the problem. A good data scientist uses this lifecycle as a guide, not as a rigid checklist.
